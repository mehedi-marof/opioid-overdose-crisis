America's Opioid Crisis

For twenty-five years, opioid deaths in America rose. In 2024, they fell faster than ever.

The country recorded its sharpest single-year drop in overdose deaths in 2024, a reversal so steep that researchers are still debating what caused it.

By Mehedi Marof · April 2026

Scroll to read

In 2024, opioid overdose deaths in the United States fell to 54,045, a decrease of about 25,300 from the year before, according to data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention compiled by KFF, a health-policy research group. Total drug overdose deaths fell to 79,384 — the lowest figure since 2019.

The drop was the largest in absolute and percentage terms ever recorded by the federal government. The CDC, in a May 2025 statement, said the change represented "more than 81 lives saved every day."

Provisional federal data show the decline continuing through 2025. As of an April 2026 release, the country was on track for fewer than 71,000 overdose deaths for the year — a figure not seen since before the pandemic.

The decline did not arrive without warning. Federal data show smaller drops in 2018 (down 4 percent) and 2023 (down 3 percent), but neither approached the scale of what happened in 2024.

To understand the scope of the change, it helps to look at the long arc that preceded it.

Since the opioid epidemic was declared a public health emergency in 2017, it has claimed more than half a million American lives, according to KFF analysis of CDC data. What started as a crisis driven by prescription opioids and heroin has, in recent years, been dominated by illicit synthetic fentanyl — a substance about a hundred times more potent than morphine.

The American opioid epidemic has unfolded in three waves, according to a CDC framework adopted in 2018.

The first wave began in the late 1990s, when prescription opioids — drugs including OxyContin, Vicodin, and oxycodone — were marketed for chronic pain management. According to a CDC analysis, the rate of opioid prescribing in the United States nearly quadrupled between 1999 and 2010. Deaths followed the prescriptions.

The second wave began around 2010. Reformulations of OxyContin and crackdowns on so-called pill mills closed off the legal supply for many people who had become dependent on prescription opioids. Many of those people moved to heroin, which was cheaper and increasingly available. Heroin deaths tripled between 2010 and 2014.

The third wave began around 2013, when illicitly manufactured fentanyl entered the American drug supply, first as an adulterant in heroin, then in counterfeit pills, then as the supply itself. By 2016, synthetic opioids had overtaken every other category as the leading driver of overdose deaths. In 2022, fentanyl-involved deaths reached a peak of approximately 73,800.

Each wave was driven down by a combination of policy, enforcement, and supply shifts. Each was followed by a more potent substance.

The geography of the epidemic moved with the substance.

The first wave concentrated in Appalachia and parts of the Mountain West. By 2004, West Virginia had the highest opioid death rate in the country, a position the state held, with brief exceptions, for most of the next two decades. The second wave moved into the Northeast and Midwest. The heroin supply followed interstate corridors out of the Appalachian basin into Ohio, Pennsylvania, and New England.

The third wave reached different regions at different times. New England saw fentanyl arrive first; New Hampshire's opioid death rate jumped sharply in 2015. The Pacific Northwest and Alaska saw fentanyl arrive last, an unevenness that public-health researchers say is now visible in the recovery.

The 2024 drop was not a single national event. It was fifty different events with different shapes.

Forty-five states recorded fewer opioid deaths in 2024 than in 2023, according to KFF analysis of CDC data. Three states — South Dakota, Alaska, and Nevada — recorded slight increases. The states with the largest declines were generally those that had been hit hardest the longest. West Virginia's opioid death rate fell from 70 per 100,000 in 2020 to 38 in 2024, a 46 percent decline. Virginia and Wisconsin each fell 44 percent.

But the picture changes when the comparison year is moved to 2019, before the pandemic surge. Measured against pre-pandemic levels, only about half of states have returned to where they were, KFF found. Alaska's 2024 opioid death rate is more than double its 2019 figure. Oregon and Washington are roughly twice as high.

About half of states still have opioid death rates above 2019 levels.

The decline was not distributed equally across the populations inside it.

According to KFF analysis, every demographic group recorded fewer opioid deaths in 2024 than in 2023. Young adults aged 18 to 25 saw the largest decline — 42 percent — and now have a death rate lower than they did in 2019. Black Americans saw a 39 percent decline, the steepest of any racial or ethnic group, after years of disproportionate loss.

Other groups did not fare as well.

American Indian and Alaska Native people had the highest opioid death rate in the country in 2024 at 35.5 per 100,000, KFF reported — about 56 percent above the national average. Their decline from 2023 was the smallest of any racial group, 28 percent. Measured against 2019, the AIAN opioid death rate has roughly doubled, up 101 percent.

Adults age 65 and older saw the smallest single-year decline of any age group, 20 percent, and their 2024 rate remained 63 percent above the 2019 baseline. KFF analysts said the slower recovery in older adults may reflect that substance use disorder is harder to detect in this group, and that few treatment programs are tailored to their needs.

Some groups remain far above their 2019 levels.

The CDC has cited five overlapping factors in the 2024 decline: expanded access to naloxone; harm-reduction programs including fentanyl test strips and syringe services; improved access to medications for opioid use disorder; law enforcement efforts targeting fentanyl trafficking; and shifts in the illicit drug supply.

A peer-reviewed analysis published in medRxiv in late 2025 argued that decreased fentanyl potency, documented in Drug Enforcement Administration seizure data, was the primary driver. Other researchers, including those at the Vital Strategies public health organization, have argued that expanded harm-reduction infrastructure deserves more credit. The CDC, in its official statements, has declined to differentiate the relative contributions.

A separate factor, less often cited in public statements but documented in the academic literature, is the size of the population at risk. A 2025 study published in The Lancet Regional Health Americas estimated that 109,783 additional opioid overdose deaths would have occurred between 2016 and 2023 had the at-risk population not declined.

The decline is also fragile, KFF analysts wrote in February 2026. Federal harm-reduction funding has been reduced under the Trump administration's 2026 budget, and the administration has redesignated illicit fentanyl as a "weapon of mass destruction," signaling a shift toward enforcement-focused responses. New synthetic opioids, including nitazenes — which the DEA has reported can be ten times more potent than fentanyl — have begun to appear in seizure data.

For now, federal data show the wave receding. Provisional 2025 figures suggest the decline is continuing, though more slowly. Whether 2024 marks the beginning of a sustained reversal will become clearer when 2025 data are finalized in late 2026.

Notes on the data

All death-certificate counts in this piece come from the National Vital Statistics System, processed by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and compiled by KFF for cross-state comparability. Rates are age-adjusted to the 2000 U.S. standard population. Drug overdose deaths are identified using ICD-10 underlying-cause codes X40–X44, X60–X64, X85, and Y10–Y14. Multiple-cause-of-death codes T40.0 through T40.4 and T40.6 are used to attribute deaths to specific opioid categories.

The State Unintentional Drug Overdose Reporting System (SUDORS) data used in this piece represents 43 reporting jurisdictions. California, Florida, Texas, Idaho, Louisiana, and North Dakota are not included.

Limits

Death certificates undercount overdose deaths in jurisdictions with fewer trained medical examiners. Race and ethnicity are coded post-mortem and are known to misclassify Native American and multiracial decedents — meaning the AIAN figures cited in this piece, already the highest of any group, are likely undercounts. Illegally manufactured fentanyl and pharmaceutical fentanyl share an ICD-10 code; only SUDORS toxicology can distinguish them.
